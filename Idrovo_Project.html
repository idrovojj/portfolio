The Storm Drain Project

Honors Chemistry
October 2017

Water is vital. Animals, plants, and humans are dependant on it. However, it is not easy to obtain clean water. Although there are storm drains and sewage systems implemented in a city, it is contaminated with a variety of different substances. These substances pose a threat to the purity of the water. In the scenario of the project, the United States Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) hired our company, The Storm Drainers, to investigate and identify all of the components of waste that end up in the storm drain. They provided us with a 5,000 gallon tanker truck filled with storm drain water mixed with its impurities. We took a small sample from the tanker, in order to identify the components more easily.

One of my most important roles in the project was to extract the sodium chloride from the remaining liquid from the sample. Extreme precision and caution were required in order to achieve this, because it was crucial that no substance was left behind during transfers from beaker to beaker. If rushed through the procedure, it was more likely to lose mass of substances, therefore lowering the obtained result of sodium chloride, and increasing the percent error. My methodical skills were challenged as I slowly navigated through the procedure. After evaporating the water and finding the mass of the sodium chloride, I was only 0.55 grams off of the actual result, which means a 89 percent recovery. Another role I was assigned during the project was to compile all of the data from all of the other materials in the storm drain sample. I was required to establish clear communication with all of my colleagues in order to be able to analyze the seven different masses of materials and perform percent error calculations for each. The most important skills further developed throughout this project were my methodical and communication skills.
